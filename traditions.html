Traditions

Lithuania and Brazil—two countries so far away from each other, so different, so exotic in their own way. Here, we would like to make you a little acquainted with the two countries we are from and the wedding traditions that distinguish (or unite) us.

Lithuania

Lithuania is a small but proud country in the geographical center of Europe. As there are not many Lithuanians in the world (approximately three million), they live surrounded by forests and lakes and always feel connection with the nature (like a tribe in Amazonia?).

Maybe due to connection to the nature, Lithuanians have also kept their ancient traditions, including pagan rituals and the Lithuanian language.

Brazil

Brazil is unique in several ways: it is the fifth largest country in the world, 1.65× larger than Europe, but it is only 518 years old; its population is composed of native-americans, descendants of africans, europeans (even Lithuanians!) and asians; it hosts several natural wonders such as Amazon Forest, Pantanal, Iguaçu Falls, Lençóis Maranhenses; it has 7.500 km of coastline and so many and diverse cultures that it is impossible to talk about Brazil as a whole—it is more like a continent! (Still, we officially speak a single language, Brazilian Portuguese, not Spanish!)

Wedding Traditions

Although weddings have many common traditions around the world, we’d like to mention several traditions that are special for the countries of Lithuania and Brazil.

Lithuanian Wedding

Lithuania have deep historical and cultural roots. The wedding is no exception: mixed with paganism and christianity, Lithuanian weddings used to follow a list of rituals and even challenges that needs to be overcome to ensure a happy marriage. You can read a little about the traditional way of celebrating Lithuanian weddings here and here (in English), or watch a little introduction video. Also, you can also watch how weddings with pagan traditions looks like.

Although there are some couples that still follow the traditional routine, nowadays weddings are more modern and include just some parts of ancient traditions. Let’s mention some of them (that you may encounter during our wedding):

Greet the newlyweds with water, bread and salt
From old times bread symbolizes material wealth and well-being. When parents greet the newlyweds with bread, they are wishing that their new home would always have bread, and thus would not lack material virtues. Salt for centuries was a symbol of longevity - salt do not stall and make other food stay fresh. Thus, giving salt, parents are wishing their love to be long-lasting and that the newlyweds would have a long and healthy life. Water, finally, symbolizes life, new beginning, as water is a foreground of every life-being. Giving water parents are greeting with a new family life and are wishing it to get bigger soon.
Family fire
The ceremony of transferring family fire is an ancient pagan tradition when fire had a strong symbolic meaning. It symbolized home well-being, warmth and safety, thus every home should always have a sacred fire burning. During the wedding, parents are sharing their home fire and giving it for the new family, new home.
Amber ceremony
Again, amber ceremony involves fire as inseparable part of life. Ancient lithuanians had seen fire as an invisible living-being, not just as a process of burning. Amber, on the other hand, was seen as a magical substance. It is said that amber is a talisman of happiness, health and love, so it guarantees happy marriage.
Something old, something new, something blue, something borrowed and a silver coin
It is quite common that a bride would have something old, something new, something blue, something borrowed on her dress, and a silver coin in her shoe. It is said that something old symbolizes connection with her family and relatives. Something new - is a symbol of a new beginning, new family. Something blue stands for faithfulness, whereas the coin in a shoe is an assurance that the new family would never lack resources.

Brazilian Wedding

Brazilian wedding traditions are not as old as Lithuanian ones, but they are good representatives of the countries’ history, joyful temperament and superstition.

Most, if not all, of those traditions have origins in the rural or workers social circles, but they are omnipresent in the Brazilian weddings.

In case you are interest in reading more about it, here and here are good references.

In our wedding, you may encounter some of those traditions:

Table of Brazilian sweets
Brazilians are known to be sweet-teeth, probably because of the country’s history around sugarcane trade and the great quantity of different sweets’ recipes. For this reason, the sweets’ table is very special in Brazilian weddings, not only because of the amount of sweets, but also because of their particular decoration style. It is an unique and delicious tradition they are proud of!
Give “bem-casado” sweet to people
“Bem-casado”, which could be translated as “well-married”, is a traditional Brazilian candy served (almost) exclusively at wedding occasions. The way it is made symbolizes the union of two people with a good layer of complicity and respect. It is said the every person who tastes it gives a special bless to the newlyweds marriage. So taste it!
Bride's shoe and groom's tie auction
That’s a quite recent tradition that come as a support for the honeymoon: during the wedding reception, a single shoe of the bride is placed in the middle of the dancing floor (or goes table by table) and the guests, at will, collaborate (chip-in) with some cash supposedly to support the newlyweds honeymoon costs—all organized by the bridesmaids. In the case of the groom’s tie, the guests “buy” pieces of the tie of the groom (yes, sometimes they cut-out pieces of the tie ), which is actioned by the groomsmen among the guests, also to collaborate with the honeymoon’s costs.
Write the names of single friends in the dress hem
It is a tradition that it is said to have started as a joke: to have the name written in the bride’s hem increases the chance to find a good husband. And the names are in the inside part of the dress because Brazilian women don’t want to look desperate for husbands.
Tame the donkey
The equivalent to chopping wood in Lithuania's traditions, that’s an old tradition from some rural areas of Brazil. The groom should tame a donkey, essential animal for farm work, but also very stubborn, to prove its manhood and suitability to be a responsible husband and provider. For obvious reasons, it is an outdated tradition, changed by extensive interrogations to the groom, performed by the parents-in-law to-be, about his work stability.
